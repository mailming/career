# Negotiation + Offer Playbook (Month 3)

Goal: 2+ offers in flight before accepting; total comp within 10-15% of top of band for the level; the role you actually want, not just the one that closed first.

## Comp data sources

Pull these before any offer arrives. Don't wait until you have a number on the table.

- **levels.fyi** — primary source. Get Staff and Senior Staff bands for each target.
- **Glassdoor** — secondary; trust the levels.fyi data more.
- **H1B / OFLC public data** — base-only floors for many companies (since they're filed with the government).
- **Blind** — community salary data, especially for AI-native shops.
- **Conversations from the referral network** — single most reliable source for AI-native shops where public data is thin.

Capture per company in a small sheet:

```
Company | Level                | Base low/mid/high | Equity vesting | Bonus | Sign-on | Notes
```

## Pre-offer signals to watch

- Recruiter asks for **current compensation** — never share. Share **expectations** if they push.
- Recruiter asks for **competing offers** — vague is fine ("a few late-stage processes"). Specific is dangerous unless the timeline genuinely overlaps.
- HM asks **how soon could you start** — buys you info on their urgency, which affects negotiation leverage.
- Level discussion happens — *always* clarify which level the offer is at and ask about the level band publicly available where possible.

## When the offer arrives

1. **Express enthusiasm.** Do not negotiate during the call.
2. Ask for the offer **in writing** with: base, equity grant (count + strike/fair-value + vesting schedule + cliff), sign-on, target bonus, level/title, start date, benefits summary.
3. Ask "when do you need a decision by?" If they say "ASAP," it's usually negotiable to "by [date 2 weeks out]."
4. Hang up; go think.

## The deliberation window

Within 24 hours of receiving the offer, write a 1-pager *for yourself*:

```
Company:
Role:
Level:
Total comp Y1 / Y4:
Equity vehicle (RSU / ISO / NSO / private):
Vesting (cliff, schedule, refresh policy):
Team (manager, peers, reports):
Closest fit to OpenClaw / MCP / Cursor / Inference work:  (1-10)
Closest fit to my 2-year goal (what is it?):  (1-10)
What I'd give up to take this:
What I'd give up by not taking it:
Worst-case (12 months in, what does failure look like?):
Best-case:
```

If you can't write the best/worst cases honestly, you don't know enough about the role yet — get on another call.

## The ask

Once you have at least one offer in hand and (ideally) one other late-stage process running:

- Open with **base**: "I was hoping we could get base closer to $X" — pick a number that's the top of band or just above.
- If they push back, move to **equity**: "Could you stretch the grant by Y RSUs / Z%?" Equity is usually where the room is at AI-native shops.
- **Sign-on** is the third lever — they have discretion here.
- Always anchor on a *reason*: "Based on the level band data I'm seeing for [this scope] at [comparable companies] and the cross-cutting nature of this role" — not "another company offered me more" unless that's actually true.

## With competing offers

If you have a second offer:

- Tell each recruiter you have **another late-stage process**; don't share the company name unless required, and even then only the offer details, not the company.
- Set a synchronized **decision-by date** that gives you a small comparison window (3-5 business days post-final-offer).
- It's OK to ask Company A to match a meaningful gap from Company B; it's not OK to imagine Company B exists.

## Counter-offers from current employer (GEICO)

Likely. Plan ahead:

- The right reason to leave is rarely just money. If the new role is the right scope + the right team + the right product surface, a counter-offer doesn't change those.
- Counter-offers from the current employer to retain you are statistically a bad signal — within 12 months you usually want to leave anyway.
- That said, money still talks for major life situations. Decide your "would not leave for less than $X" number *before* the counter arrives so you're not negotiating against yourself.

## Pre-accept checklist

Before you sign:

- [ ] Offer letter signed by them, not just sent in plain email.
- [ ] Vesting schedule + cliff confirmed.
- [ ] Refresh / merit-cycle policy understood.
- [ ] Start date confirmed (give yourself 2-4 weeks off if at all possible — you'll need it).
- [ ] Manager check: have a 1:1 with the actual manager, not just HM. Confirm reporting line and first-90-days expectations.
- [ ] Peer check: have a quick chat with 1-2 future peers (the recruiter can usually arrange).
- [ ] Background-check + employment-verification consent (these can delay starts).
- [ ] Notice at GEICO planned (2 weeks standard; do it cleanly — the AI / platform community is small).

## Two-offers-in-hand decision matrix

Don't pick on a single axis. Score each offer on:

| Dimension | Weight | Offer A | Offer B |
|-----------|--------|---------|---------|
| Total comp Y1 | 1 | | |
| Total comp Y4 (with equity assumptions) | 2 | | |
| Manager quality | 3 | | |
| Team quality | 2 | | |
| Distance from product / model | 2 (high if AI infra) | | |
| Scope / autonomy | 2 | | |
| Learning rate Y1 | 2 | | |
| Optionality at exit (what's the next 2-year job after this?) | 1 | | |

Multiply weight × score (1-10) per dimension, sum, compare. The number isn't the decision; the conversation forced by the discrepancies between your gut and the number is.

## After accepting

- Update LinkedIn after start date (or 24-48 hours after accepting, depending on your comfort — internal-only setting is fine until then).
- Email everyone in the referral network with a short thank-you and the company you landed at.
- Close out other open processes politely; recruiters remember.

## Don't accept a role you'd be embarrassed to talk about in 12 months

The thing you most want to avoid is the offer that closes first being the offer you accept by default. Build the pipeline so multiple offers can land in the same window. That's what Month 1 + Month 2 of this plan are for.
